The World Wide Web

  • The World Wide Web (WWW) is a system of interlinked documents and resources that can be accessed over the internet using web browsers. Invented by Tim Berners‑Lee in 1989, it relies on protocols like HTTP to deliver content from servers to clients. The WWW is built on the idea of hypertext — documents connected by hyperlinks — allowing users to move seamlessly from one page to another. Unlike the internet itself, which is the physical infrastructure of cables, satellites, and networks, the WWW is a service that runs on top of it, making information globally accessible in a structured way.
  • The role of the WWW in internet technology is to provide a universal platform for communication, commerce, education, and entertainment. It enables websites, applications, and services to be shared across the globe, transforming the internet into a usable environment for everyday life. The WWW has evolved through generations: Web 1.0 (static pages), Web 2.0 (interactive and social), Web 3.0 (semantic and personalized), and Web 4.0 (intelligent and autonomous). Each stage has expanded the web’s capabilities, but the core idea remains the same — connecting people and information through a vast network of linked resources.

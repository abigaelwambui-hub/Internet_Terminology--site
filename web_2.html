Web 2.0

  • Web 2.0, often called the “read‑write web,” marked a major shift in how people used the internet. Unlike Web 1.0’s static pages, Web 2.0 introduced dynamic, interactive websites where users could contribute content instead of just consuming it. This era saw the rise of blogs, wikis, social media platforms, and video‑sharing sites. Technologies like AJAX and JavaScript made pages more responsive, allowing real‑time updates without reloading. The focus moved from one‑way publishing to two‑way communication, enabling collaboration, sharing, and community building across the globe.
  • The defining feature of Web 2.0 was user participation. Platforms like Facebook, YouTube, and Wikipedia thrived because they relied on content created by their users. Personalization also became common, with websites tailoring experiences based on user preferences and behavior. This era transformed the web into a social and interactive space, paving the way for e‑commerce, online communities, and the explosion of digital media. In short, Web 2.0 turned the internet into a participatory ecosystem where everyone could be both a consumer and a creator.

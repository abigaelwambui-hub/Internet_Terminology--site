Web 1.0

  • Web 1.0, often called the “read‑only web,” was the earliest stage of the World Wide Web, spanning roughly from the early 1990s to the early 2000s. Websites during this era were static, meaning they displayed fixed information without interactive features. Content was created and published by a small number of developers or organizations, and users could only consume what was presented — much like reading a digital brochure. Pages were built with basic HTML, often text‑heavy, with minimal images, no multimedia integration, and very limited design flexibility. Navigation was simple, usually through hyperlinks, and there was little to no personalization or dynamic content.
  • The defining characteristic of Web 1.0 was its one‑way communication model: information flowed from the publisher to the user, but users had no way to contribute or interact. There were no social media platforms, comment sections, or collaborative tools. Search engines were basic, and finding information often required knowing the exact URL. Despite these limitations, Web 1.0 laid the foundation for the internet by making global information accessible for the first time. It introduced the concept of interconnected documents and demonstrated the potential of the web as a universal information system, paving the way for the more interactive and user‑driven Web 2.0.
